Can White Noise Really Help You Sleep Better? What the Research Says

Some nights, the problem is not falling asleep—it is staying asleep when a neighbor slams a door or traffic spikes outside your window. White noise is one of the most common fixes people try. The idea is simple: fill the room with a steady, neutral sound so sudden noises feel less jarring.

But does it actually work? Sleep researchers have studied white noise for decades, and the answer is more nuanced than a yes or no. Here is what the evidence suggests—and how to use sound safely if you want to try it.

What is white noise?

White noise is often described as the static you hear when a radio is not tuned to a station. Technically, it contains the full range of frequencies humans can hear, at roughly equal intensity. In everyday life, similar steady sounds include a fan, an air purifier, or a dedicated sound machine set to a soft hiss.

Because white noise creates a continuous acoustic background, brief spikes—car horns, hallway voices, a pet in another room—stand out less against that baseline.

Can white noise help you sleep?

Studies have produced mixed results. Some research finds that background noise helps people fall asleep faster and wake less often, especially when outside sounds are unpredictable. One line of explanation is sound masking: the steady noise hides disturbances. Another is a learned sleep cue—your brain associates the sound with bedtime.

Research in infants and young children has also reported lower heart and breathing rates with white noise, which may help some children settle. That does not mean the same effect applies to every adult or every night.

At the same time, a systematic review in Sleep Medicine Reviews concluded that white noise had little overall benefit for sleep quality in the studies it analyzed. Real life is messier than a lab: bedroom layout, baseline noise levels, and individual sensitivity all matter.

White noise is a comfort tool, not a treatment for insomnia, sleep apnea, or other sleep disorders. If poor sleep persists for weeks, talk to a clinician rather than turning the volume up.

Possible downsides: volume and sleep stages

Louder is not better. Sleep specialists note that noise played too loudly may interfere with important sleep stages, including deep sleep and REM sleep, and could affect hearing over time if exposure is excessive.

Practical guidelines from sleep medicine experts include:

  • Place the machine in a corner of the bedroom, not right next to your head.
  • Keep volume no louder than a quiet background conversation—if you must raise your voice to be heard over it, it is probably too loud.
  • Start the sound when you get into bed, not hours earlier.
  • If your device has a timer, experiment with playing noise only while you fall asleep instead of all night.

Pink noise and brown noise: other “colors” of sound

White noise is the best known option, but it is not the only one used for sleep.

Pink noise

Pink noise emphasizes lower frequencies, so it often sounds deeper and softer than pure white noise—think steady rain or a distant waterfall. Some studies suggest pink noise may support deep sleep in older adults and improve next-day cognitive performance, but much of that work used carefully timed audio in controlled settings. More everyday research is still needed.

Brown noise

Brown noise (sometimes called red noise) reduces high frequencies even further. People describe it as rougher or rumblier—closer to ocean surf than gentle rain. It is popular among people who find bright static irritating, though long-term sleep benefits are not yet well established.

For pink and brown noise, many clinicians recommend treating them like white noise: use a moderate volume, try a timer, and stop if sleep does not improve after a consistent trial.

How to test white noise without overdoing it

If you want to see whether sound helps you, run a simple one-week experiment:

  1. Pick one steady sound—fan, soft hiss, rain, or brown noise—and keep it the same each night.
  2. Set volume low before you lie down; resist “volume creep” on restless nights.
  3. Track sleep subjectively: time to fall asleep, night awakenings, morning alertness.
  4. Combine sound with basics: consistent schedule, dark room, and a screen-free wind-down.

Apps and bedside machines make it easy to switch sounds and set timers. The goal is a sound that disappears into the background—not one you actively listen to.

Key takeaways

  • White noise may help some people by masking outside sounds and signaling bedtime, but research results are mixed.
  • Keep volume modest, place the source away from the bed, and consider a timer instead of all-night playback.
  • Pink and brown noise are useful alternatives if pure white noise feels harsh.
  • Persistent sleep problems deserve medical evaluation—sound alone is not a cure.
